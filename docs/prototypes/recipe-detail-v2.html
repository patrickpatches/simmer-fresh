Before you start
Carbonara scrambles above 70°C. The entire dish hinges on one moment — pasta off heat, sauce on immediately. Read the full recipe before you touch the stove.
This is a two-person dish maximum in a home kitchen. Scaling up means more pasta mass, which holds heat longer and narrows your off-heat window.
No cream. Not ever. Not a little. The sauce is eggs + cheese + pasta water. Cream is a different dish.

Mise en place
Do this before you heat anything
0 / 7 done
Grate Pecorino Romano finely — not shredded, grated. Microplane preferred. Set aside.
Separate 3 egg yolks + crack 1 whole egg into a bowl. Whisk with the Pecorino and cracked pepper until thick and pale. This is your sauce.
Cut guanciale into 1cm cubes or strips. Keep at room temperature — cold fat renders unevenly.
Get a large pot of water on to boil. Salt it generously — it should taste like the sea.
Have a ladle or heatproof cup ready beside the pot — scoop 200ml of pasta water before you drain. Once the pasta is out, the water goes fast.
Set serving bowls on the bench. Warm them briefly under the hot tap and dry — cold plates set the sauce the moment it hits.
Have the rendered guanciale off-heat and ready to add back in. It goes into the pan with the pasta, before the egg mixture — don't skip this step.

Cook steps
5 steps
🥚
Step 1 · Egg sauce
📷 Photos soon
1
Mix the sauce off-heat
Whisk yolks, whole egg, and Pecorino into a thick paste. Add cracked pepper. This is your sauce — make it before anything else.
Why
Cold eggs mixed with cold cheese form a stable emulsion. Mixing it warm causes the eggs to start cooking before they hit the pasta, and you'll get scrambled eggs instead of silk.
🥓
Step 2 · Render guanciale
📷 Photos soon
2
Render the guanciale
⏱ 8 min
Cook guanciale cubes in a cold, dry pan over medium heat. The fat renders slowly and the meat crisps without burning. Remove and leave the fat in the pan.
Why
Starting cold renders the fat gradually — starting hot burns the outside before the inside renders. The rendered fat is your cooking medium for the whole dish.
🍝
Step 3 · Pasta water
📷 Photos soon
3
Cook pasta in well-salted water
Cook pasta 2 minutes short of package time — it finishes cooking in the sauce. Reserve 200ml of pasta water before draining.
Why
Pasta water is starch and salt. It's the only liquid that can loosen carbonara sauce without breaking it. Plain water dilutes and ruins it.
⚡
Step 4 · The critical step
📷 Photos soon
4
Combine off heat — this is the critical step
Add drained pasta to the guanciale pan. Take the pan completely off heat. Add egg mixture, toss constantly, adding pasta water a splash at a time until you have a creamy sauce that coats every strand. If it seizes, add more water.
Why
Eggs scramble above 70°C. Off heat, the pasta's residual heat (~65°C) is exactly right to cook the eggs without curdling. Adding water regulates the temperature and creates the emulsion. This is why carbonara has a reputation — the temperature window is narrow.
🍽
Step 5 · Plate and serve
📷 Photos soon
5
Serve immediately
Plate immediately, top with extra Pecorino and more black pepper. Carbonara does not wait — it thickens rapidly as it cools.
Finishing & tasting
The sauce should coat the back of a spoon and flow slowly when you tip the plate. If it's too thick, it seized — add a splash of warm pasta water and toss off heat again. Taste for nothing: the guanciale and Pecorino carry all the salt this dish needs. More pepper is almost always the right call.
📦
Leftovers & storage
Carbonara doesn't store or reheat gracefully — the egg sauce seizes in the fridge and reheating scrambles it. This is a cook-and-eat dish. If you have leftover pasta, use it in a frittata tomorrow: fry it in olive oil with a beaten egg and whatever's in the fridge.

Design rationale

What changed, what stayed, and the cognitive reasoning behind each section treatment.

At a glance — why this shape

Five facts, five icons, one horizontal scan. Under 2 seconds to read the whole row. The "leftovers: ✕" is low-prominence by design — it's a planning signal, not a warning. Gold is reserved for the start-cooking CTA; the glance row uses neutral weights.

The active vs total time split is important: total time (35 min) sets expectations; active time (20 min) tells you how much of that is hands-on. A 3-hour braise with 20 minutes active feels very different to a 3-hour braise with 2 hours active.

What to know — blue, not gold

Blue (#5B8FD4) is used instead of gold deliberately. Gold = action (the CTA). Blue = information or caution. The "before you start" block is not something to tap — it's something to read. The left border signals: this is a different type of content from the ingredient rows.

Placement above the ingredient list is intentional — the cook needs to know the egg-temperature constraint before they even pick up the guanciale.

Mise en place — different cognitive mode

The cook reads mise en place with the fridge door open and their hands free. They read cook steps with a pan on the stove and wet hands. Those are different contexts and the visual treatment reflects that: lighter type (--ink-soft not --ink), ochre accent (warm but not urgent), tappable checkboxes, no photos, compact line height.

The progress counter ("2 / 4 done") is a practical affordance — it answers "how far through prep am I?" without requiring the cook to read each item again.

Cook steps — unchanged by design

The existing step card design (photo + numbered step + why-note callout) works. The why-notes are a genuine competitive differentiator — Yummly doesn't explain why; Hone does. The gold left-border on the why-note is correct: it's the brand's voice interjecting.

Step 4 gets a subtle gold border on the whole card because it's the critical step. That's the only step-specific exception — the rest are identical.

Finishing & tasting — warm brown, not gold

The finishing block uses a warm-brown (#C4A882) accent instead of the blue used in "what to know." Blue is caution; warm brown is warmth and conclusion. The italic chef voice fits here — this is the chef telling you how to know the dish is right before you plate it.

Leftovers — low surface, muted copy

The leftovers block is deliberately quiet. Not every recipe has leftovers worth celebrating. For Carbonara, the honest note is "this doesn't keep — use it in a frittata." That's more useful than empty space, but it doesn't need to shout. Dark surface, muted text, no icon accents.
