# Hone Shoot — Pre-Flight Checklist

> One page. Print this or open it on a tablet — **not the phone you're shooting with**.
> Run through it start to finish before the first shot of the day.

---

## The night before

- [ ] Read the shot list for tomorrow's two recipes end to end
- [ ] Check you have every ingredient, including the garnishes (fresh herbs wilt; don't skip the list)
- [ ] Pull out props and surfaces — don't hunt for a dark slate board at 9am mid-cook
- [ ] Check phone storage: aim for at least 8 GB free. Transfer or delete what you can tonight
- [ ] Fully charge the phone overnight

---

## Morning of the shoot — before you cook anything

### Light
- [ ] Identify your window. You want indirect daylight — not direct sun hitting the food (harsh shadows) and not a room where the window is behind you (flat, dull). Side-window light is the goal.
- [ ] Check what the light does between 8am and 11am. That's your shoot window. After midday Australian light gets harsh.
- [ ] Set up your surface (dark slate/board/linen) about 60–90 cm from the window at a 45° angle to it.
- [ ] Place the white foam-core fill on the opposite side from the window, roughly 30 cm from the surface. Angle it slightly toward the food. This lifts the shadow side without eliminating it — you want dimension, not a flat studio look.

### Camera setup
- [ ] Mount on tripod. Lock it in overhead (straight down) for mise-en-place and stage shots. Lock it at 30° (roughly one arm's length above and tilted down) for hero shots.
- [ ] **Take a white-balance reference frame first.** Place a sheet of plain white paper on your surface and shoot one frame. Label it "WB ref — [date]" in your camera roll. This is the reference for the post-processing preset.
- [ ] Turn off HDR mode. HDR processing (on by default on Pixel and iPhone) combines multiple exposures and flattens contrast in ways the preset can't recover cleanly. Shoot in standard mode.
- [ ] Set grid lines on. Rule of thirds helps with hero plating composition.

### Phone housekeeping
- [ ] Pause iCloud / Google Photos auto-sync (drains battery and sometimes locks the photo roll during burst shots)
- [ ] Turn on Do Not Disturb
- [ ] Plug in a portable battery if you have one — a long cook-and-shoot session will drain the phone

---

## Surface and prop checklist (Dark Dramatic direction)

The app background is near-black (#111111). Food shot against dark surfaces integrates into the UI with zero visual clash. Warm-coloured food (golden schnitzel, butter chicken sauce, pavlova cream) pops hardest against dark grounds.

**Preferred (first choice where the dish suits it):**
- Matte black slate board — primary surface for savoury dishes
- Charcoal grey linen — for bowls and dishes where a fabric ground reads better
- Raw dark timber (oiled not varnished) — works especially for rustic dishes (roast chicken, burger)
- Brushed/matte dark steel pan or cast iron — double as cookware and surface

**Acceptable:**
- Natural stone (grey-brown marble or granite) — good for pastry and plated desserts
- Dark terracotta — works for Levantine and Indian dishes

**Avoid:**
- White ceramic plates as the primary surface (use them as serving ware only, and only if the food has dark or vivid colour)
- Bright-pine or blonde-wood boards — fight the app palette
- Busy printed linens — compete with the food
- Anything shiny/reflective — phone camera creates a flare circle you can't remove in post

**Plates and bowls:**
- Matte black or charcoal stoneware: default choice
- Off-white/bone stoneware: acceptable for very dark dishes (stew, rendang) where you need contrast
- No white porcelain dinner plates — too clinical, breaks the visual language

## During the shoot

- [ ] Cook the recipe **once through completely first** if you've never shot it before. Then cook it a second time while shooting. A confident cook produces better stage shots.
- [ ] Keep a small notebook or sticky note for each stage: "step 3 — onions translucent" so you don't miss a shot while focused on the pan
- [ ] **Shoot each stage shot within 10 seconds of hitting the "done" state.** Onions keep cooking. Sauce keeps reducing. The carbonara starts to clump. The shot window is short.
- [ ] For each shot: 3–5 frames minimum. The phone will auto-focus slightly differently on each. Pick the sharpest one in edit.
- [ ] After plating the hero shot: turn off all kitchen overhead lights. The window light only. Overhead fluorescent kills the mood and adds a green cast.

---

## Shot priority — if time runs short

Every recipe has **must-have** shots and **nice-to-have** shots. The shot lists mark these clearly.

If you're running behind:
1. Never skip the **hero shot** — it's the recipe card image
2. Never skip the **key doneness cue** — this is what makes Hone different from every other recipe app
3. Nice-to-haves (action shots, detail crops) get cut before anything else

---

## At the end of the day

- [ ] Back up the day's photos before bed (iCloud / Google Photos sync, hard drive, wherever — two copies)
- [ ] Do a quick triage pass in your camera roll: mark keepers with a star/favourite. Don't edit yet — just triage.
- [ ] Write one note (in Notes app or this doc) about what was harder than expected — the Photography Director review the following week uses this
