## 3. Description

Falafel made the right way: dried chickpeas soaked overnight, blitzed to a coarse grainy paste — not smooth — and fried until the outside cracks and the inside stays jade green. This is the street-food version of the Levant, not the café version.

---

## 4. What to Know Before You Start

1. **Dried chickpeas only.** Tinned or pre-cooked chickpeas hold too much moisture — the mixture won't bind and will fall apart in the oil. There is no shortcut here.
2. **Grainy, not smooth.** The blitz should give you a paste with visible texture, like damp sand with flecks of herb. If it looks like hummus, you've gone too far and the falafel will be dense.
3. **Test fry one first.** Before you cook the whole batch, drop one falafel into the oil. If it falls apart, the mixture needs more blitzing or a little more flour. Catch it in the pot, not on 24 falafel.

## 7. Mise en Place

- Soak the dried chickpeas in cold water for 24 hours (they'll double in size — use a big bowl). Drain and pat dry.
- Roughly chop the onion.
- Wash and dry the parsley and coriander — excess moisture is the enemy.
- Peel the garlic.
- Measure out your spices into one small bowl.
- Set up your frying station: pot, thermometer, slotted spoon, rack or paper-lined tray nearby.
- Have all your serve ingredients prepped and laid out — falafel wait for nobody.

---

## 8. Cook Steps

### Step 1 — Blitz to a coarse paste

Add the soaked, drained chickpeas to the food processor. Add the onion, garlic, parsley, coriander, cumin, ground coriander, cayenne, salt, and baking powder. Pulse — don't run it continuously. You're aiming for a texture like damp coarse sand: everything integrated, no whole chickpeas left, but not smooth paste. 

**Doneness cue:** Pinch the mixture and roll it — it should hold a rough ball without crumbling. If it crumbles, pulse a few more times. If it's completely smooth, it's gone too far; the falafel will be dense rather than light.

**If the mixture won't hold:** Add the plain flour one tablespoon at a time, pulse, and test again.

**Rest the mixture:** Transfer to a bowl, cover, and refrigerate for at least 30 minutes. This helps it firm up and makes shaping easier.

**Why:** The resting time is not just convenience — it lets the starches in the chickpea paste hydrate the flour and bind the mixture properly. Skipping it gives you fragile falafel.

---

### Step 2 — Shape

Using a falafel scoop or two tablespoons, portion the mixture into balls or slightly flattened discs (~30–35g each, roughly the size of a golf ball but slightly flattened). Don't compress them too hard — a loose-ish shape lets the inside stay open and light when it fries.

**Test fry now:** Heat your oil to 175°C and drop one falafel in. It should hold together, turn deep brown on the outside in 3–4 minutes, and come out with a crackled exterior. If it falls apart: more flour, chill longer. If it's pale and dense: oil not hot enough.

**Why:** The test fry is non-negotiable. One broken falafel in the test costs nothing. Twenty-four broken falafel in the pot is a mess.

---

### Step 3 — Fry in batches

Heat the oil to 175°C. Fry in batches of 5–6 — don't crowd the pot; crowding drops the oil temperature and you'll get greasy falafel instead of crisp ones. Lower each falafel gently with a slotted spoon.

Fry for 3–4 minutes, turning once halfway, until deep golden-brown. The outside should have a visible crack running across it — that's the moisture escaping and the crust setting.

**Doneness cue:** Deep golden-brown, slightly cracked exterior. Cut one open to check: the inside should be jade green with a light, slightly grainy texture — not white and dense, not raw-smelling.

Transfer to a rack (not paper towel — paper holds steam and softens the crust).

Allow the oil to return to 175°C between batches.

**Why:** The crack on the outside is structural — it's the starch and herb mixture setting under the heat, which locks the shape and gives the crunch. Below 170°C, the outside absorbs oil before it sets; above 185°C, the outside colours before the inside cooks through.

---

### Step 4 — Serve immediately

Falafel are best within 5 minutes of frying. Serve with warm flatbread, hummus, sliced tomato, cucumber, fresh mint, and pickles. Load the flatbread yourself at the table — don't pre-wrap.

**Why:** The crunch is perishable. Wrapping them ahead steams the crust off. Street vendors fry to order for a reason.

---

## 9. Finishing & Tasting

Taste one falafel the moment it's cool enough to eat. It should be distinctly seasoned — the outside slightly salty from the fry, the inside fragrant from the cumin and coriander. If the spice isn't coming through, a pinch more ground cumin on the next batch.

A squeeze of lemon over the falafel as you serve is not optional — it cuts the oil and lifts the herb flavour.

---

## 10. Leftovers & Storage

**Uncooked mixture:** Refrigerates for up to 48 hours. Freezes well (portion into balls, freeze on a tray, then bag). Fry from frozen — 175°C for 5–6 minutes.

**Cooked falafel:** Best eaten fresh. Leftovers reheat in a 200°C oven for 8–10 minutes — they'll crisp back up. Do not microwave (they go soggy and sad).

## Cultural Notes

Falafel is a staple street food across the Levant — Palestinian, Lebanese, Syrian, and Jordanian cuisines all have deep traditions with the dish. It is part of the daily food culture of the region, not a novelty or restaurant concept. The chickpea version (as opposed to the Egyptian fava bean version, ta'ameya) is the Levantine standard.
