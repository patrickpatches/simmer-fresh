## 3 · Description

A weeknight stir-fry done correctly is one of the fastest, most adaptable meals in a home cook's kit. This version is built around a simple savoury-sweet-umami sauce, high heat, and the principle that vegetables go in by hardness, not by preference. Get the wok or pan screaming hot and don't walk away.

---

## 4 · What to Know Before You Start

1. **Everything goes in fast once the heat's on — prep is everything.** Have every vegetable cut, every sauce measured, and the cornflour slurry made before you turn on the heat. Once the wok is hot, you have 8 minutes of continuous movement ahead of you.
2. **High heat is the whole game.** A home stove rarely gets as hot as a restaurant wok burner, which means you need to work in smaller batches. Crowding the pan drops the temperature and steams the vegetables instead of searing them — you lose the colour and the texture.
3. **Velveting makes the chicken silky.** A 10-minute soak in cornflour and soy sauce before cooking changes the texture of the chicken — it stays tender rather than turning rubbery at high heat. Don't skip it.

## 7 · Mise en Place

*(Do all of this before turning on the heat.)*

- Slice the chicken into 5 mm strips. Toss with 1 tbsp cornflour and 1 tsp soy sauce in a bowl. Set aside for 10 minutes — this is the velveting step.
- Cut all vegetables: broccoli into small florets, carrot into thin batons, capsicum into strips. Keep them grouped on the board or in separate bowls — they go in at different times.
- Slice garlic. Grate ginger. Slice spring onions, keeping whites and greens in separate piles.
- Mix the sauce in a small bowl: oyster sauce + light soy + sugar + water. Stir to dissolve sugar.
- Mix the slurry in another small bowl: 1 tsp cornflour + 2 tbsp cold water. Stir until no lumps.
- Start the rice if using — stir-fry moves fast and the rice needs to be ready when the wok finishes.
- Set everything within arm's reach of the hob. You won't have time to search for anything once cooking starts.

---

## 8 · Cook Steps

### Step 1 — Sear the chicken

Get the wok or pan over the highest heat your stove can produce. Wait until it begins to smoke — not just hot, actually smoking. Add 2 tbsp of the neutral oil and swirl to coat. Add the chicken in a single layer (in batches if needed — don't crowd). Press down gently with tongs and leave undisturbed for 90 seconds.

**Doneness cue:** The underside should be golden-brown and release cleanly from the pan without sticking. Flip and cook 1 more minute. Remove chicken to a plate — it does not need to be fully cooked through at this stage; it finishes in the sauce.

**Why note:** Leaving the chicken undisturbed lets the Maillard reaction develop a crust on the surface. Moving it constantly prevents browning and results in grey, steamed chicken. The velveting cornflour coating also helps the crust develop and keeps the interior moist.

**Recovery:** If the chicken is sticking, the pan isn't hot enough — wait another 30 seconds before attempting to move it.

### Step 2 — Cook the vegetables by hardness

Without washing the pan, add the remaining 1 tbsp oil. Add the spring onion whites, garlic, and ginger. Stir constantly for 30 seconds — you want fragrant, not brown. Add the carrot and broccoli. Stir-fry 2 minutes, moving constantly. Add capsicum. Stir-fry 1 more minute.

**Doneness cue:** Broccoli should be vivid green and just tender with a slight bite — if it's dull green, it's overcooked. Carrot should have some resistance. Capsicum should still have some firmness.

**Why note:** Harder vegetables need more time at high heat. Adding everything simultaneously means some vegetables are perfect and some are mush. Carrot and broccoli are the hardest items here; capsicum is softer and cooks quickly. Spring onion whites and garlic are aromatic bases, not vegetables — they go in first to flavour the oil.

### Step 3 — Sauce and finish

Return the chicken to the wok. Pour the pre-mixed sauce over everything and toss to coat. Let it bubble for 30 seconds. Give the cornflour slurry a quick stir (the starch settles) and pour it in while stirring — the sauce will thicken within 30 seconds.

Remove from heat. Add the sesame oil and spring onion greens. Toss once.

**Doneness cue:** The sauce should coat the ingredients glossily — not watery, not gluey. It should move lazily when you tilt the pan.

**Why note:** The cornflour slurry thickens by gelatinisation — the starch granules absorb the hot liquid and swell. It happens fast and can go from thin to gluey in 20 seconds; add the slurry with one hand and stir with the other, stopping as soon as it thickens. Sesame oil is added off-heat because its aromatics evaporate immediately at cooking temperature.

## 9 · Finishing & Tasting

Serve immediately over steamed rice. Taste before plating — the sauce should be savoury and slightly sweet with a background of sesame. If it tastes flat, add a few drops more soy sauce. If it tastes one-dimensional, a few drops of rice wine vinegar (or just the natural acid of a wedge of lime) wakes it up.

The vegetables should have colour and slight bite — not soft. If they've gone soft, the heat was too low or the pan was overcrowded. Note it for next time.

---

## 10 · Leftovers & Storage

**Storage:** Refrigerates well for up to 2 days in a sealed container. The sauce will absorb into the vegetables and the texture softens — still good, just different.

**Reheating:** Wok or frying pan over high heat with a splash of water — 2 minutes, stirring constantly. Microwave works as a fallback but the vegetables will be softer.

**Freezing:** Not recommended — the vegetables lose all texture and the sauce separates.

**Variation:** Cold leftover stir-fry is surprisingly good tossed through noodles or fried rice the next day. Don't bin it.
