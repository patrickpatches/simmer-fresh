## 3 · Description

Lasagne is not a fast dish — the Bolognese ragù needs three hours on the lowest possible heat to reach the depth that makes this worth making. The work is front-loaded; the last 45 minutes are mostly assembly and baking. Make it the day before if you can: cold lasagne sets into clean layers and reheats as something noticeably better than the day it was assembled.

---

## 4 · What to Know Before You Start

1. **The ragù is the dish.** Béchamel and pasta are structural; the meat sauce is the flavour. A rushed 45-minute bolognese makes thin, acidic lasagne. Three hours on very low heat with milk added early — this is what Marcella Hazan was insisting on and she was right.
2. **Build in layers, not heaps.** Thin, even layers of sauce and béchamel between each pasta sheet. Think of it as a terrine, not a pile. Even layers = even cooking and clean cuts.
3. **Rest before cutting.** 15 minutes out of the oven before cutting. The filling sets; the layers hold. Cut into hot lasagne and it collapses into a pool. This is not a test of patience — it's part of the cooking process.

## 7 · Mise en Place

*(Do all of this before turning on the heat — the soffritto and then the ragù takes 3 hours; start early.)*

- Finely dice onion, carrot, and celery into equal-sized pieces (the soffritto). The uniformity matters — uneven pieces mean some are burnt before others are soft.
- Mince garlic.
- Open and hand-crush the tinned tomatoes into a bowl — squeezing through your fist breaks them down. Discard the juice (or keep for a pasta sauce another time — it's not needed here).
- Measure the milk and wine into separate jugs.
- For the béchamel: weigh butter and flour, measure milk. Keep these on the bench ready — béchamel needs full attention for 10 minutes.
- Grate the Parmigiano.
- If using dried lasagne sheets that require pre-soaking: soak in hot water 5 minutes before assembly. Many brands don't require this — read the packet.

## 8 · Cook Steps

### Step 1 — Build the soffritto

In the pot over medium heat, warm the olive oil. Add the onion, carrot, and celery. Cook, stirring occasionally, until the vegetables are completely soft and the onion is translucent — 10 minutes. Do not brown them.

**Doneness cue:** The soffritto should be slumped and glossy, jammy around the edges, with no raw crunch remaining.

**Why note:** The soffritto is the flavour foundation. Soft vegetables release their sugars slowly into the fat, creating a sweet, savoury base. Browning them too quickly gives a bitter note that persists through the three-hour braise.

### Step 2 — Brown the meat

Increase heat to medium-high. Add the garlic and stir 30 seconds. Add both minces, breaking up with a wooden spoon. Let each addition sit for 1 minute before stirring — you want genuine browning, not steaming. Work in batches if the pan is crowded.

**Doneness cue:** Deep brown on the outside of the meat, not grey. This takes 8–10 minutes of patience.

**Why note:** The browning (Maillard reaction) builds the deep, savoury flavour that the long braise will then dissolve into the sauce. Grey, steamed mince produces flat-tasting ragù regardless of how long it braises. This step cannot be recovered from if skipped.

### Step 3 — Milk first, then wine (Marcella's sequence)

Add the milk. Stir, scraping up any browned bits from the base. Let the milk cook off completely over medium heat — the pan should look nearly dry before the next step. This takes 8–10 minutes.

Then add the wine. Let it cook off completely in the same way — 5 minutes.

**Doneness cue:** No visible pooling liquid. The meat looks coated but not swimming.

**Why note:** Adding milk before the wine is Marcella Hazan's signature step. The milk proteins coat the meat fibres and prevent the acid in the wine and tomatoes from tightening them. The result is yielding, tender mince rather than the slightly grainy texture that wine-first ragù often produces.

### Step 4 — The long braise

Add the crushed tomatoes, bay leaves, salt and pepper. Stir to combine. Bring to a gentle simmer, then reduce heat to the lowest your stove can manage. The surface should barely blip — one or two bubbles every few seconds.

Cover partially (lid slightly ajar) and cook for 2.5–3 hours, stirring every 30 minutes. Add a splash of water if the ragù looks dry.

**Doneness cue:** The ragù should be rich, dense, and deeply coloured — a dark mahogany red. The fat will have partially separated to the surface and looks glossy. When you drag a spoon through it, the path should close slowly, not immediately.

**Why note:** Long, low heat breaks down the connective tissue in the mince and allows the Maillard compounds from the browning step to dissolve into the sauce. You cannot replicate this with higher heat and less time — the result is fundamentally different in texture and depth. This is the reason to make lasagne.

### Step 5 — Make the béchamel

In a medium saucepan over medium heat, melt the butter. Add the flour all at once and stir constantly with a wooden spoon for 2 minutes until the paste smells biscuity — not raw and not brown. This is the roux.

Remove from heat. Add the milk in three additions, whisking constantly after each until smooth before adding the next. Return to medium heat, whisking. The sauce will thicken to a consistency that coats the back of a spoon — about 5 minutes of stirring. Season with salt, white pepper, and freshly grated nutmeg.

**Doneness cue:** Coats a spoon and a line drawn through it holds for 2 seconds before flowing back. No lumps.

**Why note:** Adding all the milk at once to a hot roux creates lumps because the outer starch granules gelatinise and form a skin around unmixed flour. Three additions and constant whisking gives you a smooth sauce every time.

**Recovery:** If lumps form, remove from heat and beat vigorously with a whisk. If they persist, pass through a fine strainer.

### Step 6 — Assemble

Preheat oven to 180°C. Lightly oil the baking dish.

Layer in this order (4 layers total):
1. Thin smear of béchamel on the base (prevents the bottom sheet sticking)
2. Lasagne sheets (overlapping slightly to cover)
3. Ragù — spread evenly, not thick
4. Béchamel — spread evenly
5. Grating of Parmigiano
Repeat layers 2–5 three more times. Finish the top with a generous layer of béchamel and the remaining Parmigiano.

**Why note:** The base béchamel layer prevents the pasta from bonding to the dish. Even ragù layers matter: thick patches mean some sheets are underdone while thin patches are dried out.

### Step 7 — Bake

Bake uncovered at 180°C for 35–40 minutes until the top is golden-brown and bubbling at the edges.

**Doneness cue:** Deep golden top with dark edges. When you insert a skewer or thin knife into the centre and leave it there for 5 seconds, it should come out hot to the touch.

### Step 8 — Rest

Remove from oven. Do not cut for at least 15 minutes.

**Why note:** The filling is still liquid when it comes out of the oven. Resting allows it to set into a cohesive layer. Cut too early and the layers separate. 15 minutes is the minimum; 20 is better.

## 9 · Finishing & Tasting

Cut into squares with a sharp knife. Serve with a simple green salad and crusty bread.

Taste a corner before plating. It should taste deeply savoury with a background sweetness from the soffritto. If it tastes flat, it needs salt and possibly a thread of good olive oil over the top. If the ragù tastes acidic, the braise was too short.

The layering should hold on the plate — if it collapses into a pile, it either didn't rest long enough or the ragù was too wet.

## 10 · Leftovers & Storage

**Storage:** This is better the next day. The flavours meld overnight and the lasagne cuts into cleaner squares when cold. Refrigerate up to 4 days.

**Reheating:** Covered with foil at 160°C for 20 minutes, or individual portions in the microwave with a splash of water. The foil prevents the top from over-browning before the centre is hot.

**Freezing:** Excellent — freeze in individual portions. Thaw in the fridge overnight, reheat covered in the oven at 160°C. Texture is well-preserved because béchamel handles freezing better than tomato-only sauces.

**Ragù separately:** The bolognese ragù freezes brilliantly. If you make a double batch, freeze half as pasta sauce for another night.
