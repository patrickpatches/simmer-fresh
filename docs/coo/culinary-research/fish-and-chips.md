## 3 · Description

Beer batter fish and twice-cooked chips — the Australian Friday ritual. The batter is carbonated by the beer, light and crisp when it hits 180°C oil; the chips are par-cooked first so they can be fried fast and finish crispy throughout, not pale in the middle. This requires a thermometer and some attention. The result is worth it.

---

## 4 · What to Know Before You Start

1. **The batter needs to be cold and used quickly.** Mix it immediately before frying. Cold batter hits hot oil and the temperature shock, combined with the CO₂ in the beer, puffs the batter into a light shell. Warm batter or batter that has been resting too long makes a denser, heavier coating.
2. **Chips are twice-cooked — this is not optional.** Par-cook at 150°C to cook through without colour, then rest, then fry again at 190°C to crisp. Single-frying produces chips that are either pale in the centre or burnt on the outside. The double-cook is the technique.
3. **Get the fish as dry as possible before battering.** Moisture is the enemy of batter adhesion. Pat dry thoroughly with paper towel, then lightly dust with flour immediately before dipping. The flour gives the batter something to grip.

## 7 · Mise en Place

*(30 minutes before frying.)*

- Cut and rinse the potato chips. Dry very thoroughly — spread on a clean tea towel and pat, then leave uncovered for 10 minutes. Wet chips spit violently in hot oil and don't crisp.
- Pat the fish fillets completely dry. Refrigerate uncovered on a plate.
- Set up the oil in the pot. Do not heat yet.
- Measure the flour and baking powder into a bowl for the batter. Do not add the beer yet — this happens immediately before frying.
- Set up the draining station: wire rack over a tray next to the hob.
- Make the tartare sauce if making from scratch — this takes 5 minutes and can be done any time.

---

## 8 · Cook Steps

### Step 1 — First fry: cook the chips through (150°C)

Heat the oil to 150°C. Add chips in two batches — don't crowd the pot. Fry for 5–6 minutes until cooked through but barely coloured — they should look pale gold, still slightly soft on the outside.

Remove with tongs and spread on the rack. Leave to cool for at least 10 minutes — this rest allows the interior steam to escape and the potato to firm slightly before the second fry.

**Doneness cue:** A chip pressed between two fingers should yield without resistance (cooked through) but not collapse (not overcooked). Pale gold colour; no deep browning yet.

**Why note:** The first fry cooks the potato through at a temperature low enough to avoid browning the outside prematurely. During the rest, the surface dries further. The second fry at higher temperature then crisps the outside in the 2–3 minutes it takes — instead of the 8–10 minutes a single-fry requires, during which the interior overcooks.

### Step 2 — Make the batter (do this now, not before)

Whisk flour, baking powder, and salt together in a bowl. Add the cold beer all at once and whisk briefly — 10 seconds. Stop when it's just combined. Some small lumps are fine; overmixing develops gluten and makes the batter tough and dense. The batter should be the consistency of thin cream — it drips from the whisk in a continuous stream.

Use immediately. Do not let it stand.

**Why note:** Cold beer + baking powder + minimal mixing = light batter. Every minute of standing, the bubbles escape and the gluten relaxes into a stodgier coat. This step happens at the last possible moment.

### Step 3 — Batter and fry the fish (180°C)

Increase oil to 180°C. Dust each fish fillet lightly in plain flour, shaking off the excess. Dip into the batter, letting the excess drip off for 2 seconds, then lower gently into the oil away from you. Fry in batches of 2 maximum.

Fry 4–5 minutes, turning once at the halfway point, until deep golden-brown and cooked through.

**Doneness cue:** Deep golden batter with no pale patches. When you press the thickest part, it should feel firm with slight give — soft means the fish isn't cooked. Remove to the rack and season with salt immediately.

**Why note:** Flouring the fish before dipping gives the batter a dry surface to adhere to. Without it, the batter slides off the fish during frying. The fish cooks inside the batter shell simultaneously with the batter crisping — the temperature has to be high enough to cook both together in 4–5 minutes.

**Recovery:** If the batter is pale and greasy after 4 minutes, the oil temperature dropped when the fish went in — increase heat and give it another 2 minutes. If the batter is browning before 3 minutes and the fish isn't cooked, the oil is too hot — reduce heat and cover the pot briefly.

### Step 4 — Second fry: crisp the chips (190°C)

Increase oil to 190°C. Fry the par-cooked chips for 2–3 minutes until deep golden and crispy.

Remove immediately to the rack. Season generously with salt while hot.

**Doneness cue:** Deep golden all over, rigid and crackly when tapped — not soft. They should hold their shape when picked up.

**Why note:** The high temperature at the second fry drives off remaining surface moisture and creates the Maillard crust that is the defining texture of a good chip. The inside is already cooked from the first fry — the second fry is entirely about the exterior.

## 9 · Finishing & Tasting

Serve immediately — fish and chips are a dish of minutes. Set on plates lined with paper (the traditional presentation; it absorbs oil and keeps everything crisp for the first few minutes). Lemon quarters, tartare sauce, malt vinegar on the side.

Taste a chip: it should be salty, crisp outside, fluffy inside. If the chips are soft, the oil wasn't hot enough at the second fry or the first fry was too long.

Taste the fish batter: crisp, light, with a faint yeasty note from the beer. If it tastes greasy and dense, the batter was too warm or the oil too cool.

Add more salt than you think you need. This is one of the foods that needs it.

## 10 · Leftovers & Storage

**This dish does not keep.** Fish and chips must be eaten within 10–15 minutes of frying. Refrigerating and reheating battered fish produces a limp, pale shadow of the original. This is the defining characteristic of the dish — make it and eat it.

**If you must reheat chips:** 220°C oven, spread on a rack, 5–8 minutes. They recover reasonably well. Do not microwave.

**Leftover fish:** Remove the batter, flake the fish, mix with mayonnaise, lemon, and diced cornichons for a fish sandwich — perfectly good the next day.
