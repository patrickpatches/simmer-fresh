## 3 · Description

A bone-in leg of lamb rubbed with rosemary, garlic, and mustard, roasted hot and then slow — the high blast builds a crust; the long, lower cook renders the fat and brings the centre to pink. Rest it properly and it slices cleanly. This is the dish that defines an Australian Sunday.

---

## 4 · What to Know Before You Start

1. **One leg feeds 6–8 people — buy by weight, not by person count.** A 2–2.5 kg bone-in leg is the right size. Going to 3 kg doesn't mean serving more people — it means the outer layers are done before the centre is. If feeding more than 8, roast two legs side by side rather than one enormous one.
2. **The resting period is not optional — it is part of the cooking.** Twenty minutes minimum out of the oven, uncovered (not under foil, which steams the crust). The juices redistribute during the rest; cut immediately and they run out onto the board.
3. **Temperature beats timing.** A 2 kg leg at 180°C will be done in 70–80 minutes — but your oven, your lamb, and your preferred doneness may vary. A probe thermometer removes all guesswork: 58–60°C at the thickest part for blushing pink; 65°C for medium; 70°C for well done (not recommended).

## 7 · Mise en Place

*(30 minutes before roasting begins.)*

- Remove the lamb from the fridge 30 minutes before cooking. Cold meat in a hot oven cooks unevenly — the outside seizes while the centre stays cold.
- Preheat oven to 220°C (200°C fan-forced).
- Mix the marinade: olive oil + Dijon mustard + lemon juice + salt + pepper + half the chopped rosemary. Stir until combined.
- Using a paring knife, cut 16 small deep pockets into the lamb — 8 on each side, in the thicker sections, angled toward the bone. Push a sliver of garlic and a pinch of chopped rosemary deep into each pocket. This takes 3 minutes and contributes enormously to the final flavour.
- Rub the marinade over the entire surface of the lamb, getting into any folds.
- Scatter the remaining rosemary sprigs and whole garlic cloves in the base of the roasting tin. Place the lamb on top (on a rack if you have one).

## 8 · Cook Steps

### Step 1 — High blast for the crust

Roast at 220°C for 20 minutes. The surface should begin to brown and the kitchen should smell of caramelised lamb fat and rosemary.

**Doneness cue:** The top of the lamb is golden-brown with darkened herb patches. No pink raw surface visible.

**Why note:** The initial high heat triggers Maillard browning on the surface — this builds the flavour compounds that define a roast. Without this blast, the low-temperature braise produces grey, steamed-looking lamb. The crust also seals the surface, which reduces moisture loss during the long second phase (this is a simplification, but the practical result is correct).

### Step 2 — Lower roast to doneness

Reduce oven to 180°C (160°C fan-forced). Continue roasting until the internal temperature at the thickest part of the leg (not touching the bone) reads your target:

- **58–60°C** — blushing pink throughout. This is the preferred serve.
- **65°C** — medium, a small pink centre remaining.
- **70°C** — well done. The lamb will be drier; not recommended.

For a 2 kg leg at 180°C, this typically takes a further 50–70 minutes after the high blast.

**Doneness cue:** The thermometer is the authoritative signal. Colour alone is not reliable because leg of lamb varies in thickness. A well-browned exterior tells you the crust is right; it tells you nothing about the centre.

**Why note:** Every oven is calibrated differently; the lamb's starting temperature varies; the bone conducts heat differently at different positions. Relying on time alone produces overcooked lamb at least a third of the time. A probe thermometer is a 3-second action that eliminates uncertainty.

### Step 3 — Rest

Remove lamb from the oven. Do NOT tent with foil — foil traps steam and softens the crust you just built. Lift onto a warm board or plate and rest, uncovered, for 20 minutes minimum.

During the rest: pour off excess fat from the tin (keep a tablespoon), place tin over medium heat on the hob, and deglaze with 200 ml of water or stock, scraping up the browned bits. This is a simple jus — reduce for 5 minutes and season.

**Doneness cue:** The lamb will continue to cook internally during the rest — the centre temperature rises a further 3–5°C. Factor this in when removing from the oven (pull it at 56–57°C for a pink result).

**Why note:** Resting allows the muscle fibres, which have contracted and squeezed the juices toward the centre during cooking, to relax. Cut immediately and those juices pool on the board. Twenty minutes is the minimum for a leg this size; 30 minutes is better and the lamb stays warm enough to serve.

### Step 4 — Carve

Carve across the grain in 5 mm slices, working around the bone. The first cuts are the most exposed and will be more done; the inner slices near the bone will be the pinkest. Offer both.

**Why note:** Cutting across the grain (perpendicular to the muscle fibres) shortens the fibres in each slice, making the lamb easier to chew. Cutting with the grain produces long, stringy pieces.

## 9 · Finishing & Tasting

Arrange slices on a warmed platter with the jus poured around (not over — it softens the crust). Scatter a few fresh rosemary sprigs and the roasted garlic cloves from the tin.

Taste a slice before plating. It should be deeply savoury with a faint sweetness from the roasted garlic. If the meat tastes flat, it needs salt — add a small pinch of flaky salt to the platter. If the jus is too thin, reduce it another 2 minutes. If it's too intense, add a splash of water and a small piece of cold butter for gloss.

Traditional accompaniments: roasted vegetables cooked alongside (potatoes, parsnip, carrot added to the tin in the last 40 minutes), and a mint sauce or fresh mint jelly.

---

## 10 · Leftovers & Storage

**Storage:** Refrigerate carved slices for up to 3 days. Store with the jus poured over to prevent drying.

**Reheating:** Cover with foil, add a splash of water or stock, and warm at 160°C for 10–12 minutes. Do not reheat at high heat — the lean sections dry out quickly.

**Cold lamb:** Thinly sliced cold lamb is exceptional in a sandwich with mint sauce and dijon mustard on good bread. Better cold than many meals hot.

**Stock:** After carving, simmer the lamb bone with onion, carrot, and celery for 2 hours to make a lamb stock — excellent base for barley soup or a rich gravy.

**Freezing:** Yes — freeze sliced, with jus, for up to 3 months. Thaw overnight in the fridge.
